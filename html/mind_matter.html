Two ways of thinking

It's often said that that Japanese people have a particular affinity for understanding robots, and technology at large, because the native Shinto religion assigns a spirit to everything, even inanimate objects.

My premise is that the brain is structured to understand things in in two different ways, one psychological, one physical.

Psychological

It's well established that humans have a "Theory of Mind". Which is to say, a special reasoning process which assumes that the behaviour of others is explicable by assuming that they have a mind similar to their own. [1]

Autistic children are commonly said not to have a Theory of Mind, that is, they do not have a mental model for the way other people think. [2]

Physical

Even very young children understand the physical world in a deep way [3]. Whether this is innate or not (all the evidence seems to suggest so), it's clear that humans are intuitively good at physics. Calculating where a ball will land, and factoring in air resistance, leads to some complex maths. However, most people can catch a ball without difficulty

If I were to put this difference in an anecdote, I would say that to throw a rock at someone requires you to understand physics. To realise they might get annoyed and throw it back at you requires an understanding of mind.

Animism

Mind stuff is pervasive

Projection of psychological features onto everything, since there are few scientific ideas to make sense of the world.

  • Aboriginal "dreamtime" Cosmogony
  • Beliefs that plants, animals or places have human spirits

Implicit Dualism

Mind-stuff is pure and perfect, physical-stuff is secondary

Emergence of mathematics, deeper thought and complex mental models of the world convince physical stuff is not to be trusted.

  • Plato's theory of forms asks us to look beyond physical-stuff to the true forms - mind-stuff

Cartesian Explicit Dualism

Mind-matter and physical are formally separately identified for the first time.

Descartes was the first philosopher to make dualism explicit. His view was that since we can imagine a mind without a body, and a body without a mind, they must be two separate things.

Psychological Dualism

Science and technology erode the uniqueness of mental-matter

Properties considered unique to mental-matter are shown not to be. Despite this, we still treat the two types of matter as separate in our everyday thought.

  • Babbage and automata
  • Turing and the Turing test
  • fMRI scanning and free will
  • Spell checking

Materialism

In the future, just as humans once accepted an animistic or rationalist view of the world, we will accept that the only thing that exists is physical-stuff.

  • Dan Dennett's materialism
  • Nick Bostrom and his transhumanism
